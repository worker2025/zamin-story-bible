# V07 — Culture

## Taxation / Levy (خراج)

- The estate/palace levies goods from households: wool-thread (پشموخُمه) and fine animal oil (خیگِ روغنِ خوش), among others (grain implied throughout). Evidence — Chapter 1.
- Tax officials stamp clay seals (مُهرهای گِلی) on jars in the bazaar. Evidence — Chapter 4 — Quote: "دو مأمور خراج، مُهرهای گِلی را روی دهانه چند خمره میزدند."
- A steward (مباشر) records debtors on a tablet in the bazaar. Evidence — Chapter 4.
- Villages are shown, during the "خاموشیِ فرمان" period, refusing tax collectors and redistributing seized grain among widows and orphans themselves. Evidence — Chapter 9.

## Day-labor (نونتِلی)

- Footnote: "کارگر بدون مزد و مواجب! کار فقط به ازای سیر شدن و زنده ماندن!" (workers without wage; paid only in enough food to survive) — described waiting under toot (mulberry) trees hoping for cart-work. Evidence — Chapter 4.

## Public storytelling / oral history display

- Chishpish hangs a large painted/carved buffalo hide in the Ayapir bazaar and narrates its images to children (a falcon god, "guardian of covenants"). Evidence — Chapter 4.
- Ata, at Golshor, similarly unrolls painted hides depicting ordinary laborers to provoke discussion among the caravanserai's regulars. Evidence — Chapter 6.

## Marking of graves / memorial customs

- Nika is buried without a headstone; a broken, drawn-upon brick is placed instead. Evidence — Chapter 7.
- A bystander touches the burial oak and says: "از امروز، بلوط هم نامش را به یاد میسپارد." Evidence — Chapter 7.
- Kuru's memorial is a brick inscribed with a collectively-dictated epitaph, placed among ordinary vote-bricks rather than set apart. Evidence — Chapter 13.

## Popular assembly / شورا (shura) — governance custom that develops over the novel

- Origin reference (told by Raju, describing a custom in "سِند"): villagers gather yearly without a king to decide water division; disputes occur but are resolved because failure to agree means "سال بعد چیزی برای خوردن نداشتند." Evidence — Chapter 3.
- A second precedent is described via Halazh's coded scroll from Lahson: people gather each spring by a canal, discuss water, argue over dates, then "هر کس سهم خودش را میبرد... خودشان [تصمیم میگیرند]." Evidence — Chapter 3.
- The word "شورا" is itself explicitly discussed/half-invented in dialogue at Eshkaft-e-Salman-adjacent scenes: variously guessed as "شولْوا"، "شَوال"، "شو راه"; one pirmard defines it: "شورا یعنی بیست نفر دورِ یک کاسهی آب بنشینن و دعوا کنن که سهمِ کی بیشتره. آخرش هم از همون کاسه بخورن." Footnote clarifies: "شوربا: آش؛ تلفظ عامیانهی شوربا." (i.e. "shura" is jokingly conflated with "shorba"/soup in dialogue — an in-text pun, not an etymological claim by the archivist). Evidence — Chapter 11.
- **Formal mechanics that emerge across Ch.10–13:**
  1. Public tablet/brick placement to represent agenda items (آب، جو، راه، انبار). Chapter 11.
  2. Anonymous brick-voting: everyone places one blank brick beside one of two marked symbols (a flowing-water line = act, a horizontal line = wait); no names are read, only bricks counted. Chapter 11.
  3. Rotational duty systems: e.g., a "reciprocity" rule proposed by a woman — anyone wanting water access must also bring a shovel for canal-clearing (qanat maintenance); failing to appear without excuse means going to the "back of the line" for the next turn, or (jokingly proposed by a child) working three days on others' behalf. Chapter 12.
  4. Knot-counting rope: three girls (from Parsial, Pioun, Kaldozakh) tie a knot in a rope for each accepted brick, producing a rope described as "مثل مار چاقی از شاخه آویزان." Chapter 12.
  5. A traveling caravan later carries bricks, tablets, and knotted water-agreements to other villages to extend the process. Chapter 12–13.
- Two priests are shown privately noting that popular arbitration reduces the temple's share of judicial authority. Evidence — Chapter 11.
- A royal envoy is peacefully turned away when he insists on speaking only to "کسی که اختیار داشته باشد" (someone with authority); the crowd replies "اختیارِ آب و زمین، اینجاست." Evidence — Chapter 11.

## Songs / oral tradition

- Nika's chang-playing is repeatedly framed by others (and eventually by herself) not as ritual worship (نیایش) but as "صدای مردم" (the voice of the people). Evidence — Chapter 1.
- A lullaby, sung nightly by the unnamed girl to her younger brother, becomes the means by which she learns to play the chang publicly at Eshkaft-e-Salman. Evidence — Chapter 12.
- A shepherd's ney (نی) call recurs as a parallel "voice," described at the ceasefire scene (Ch.8) and again answering the girl's playing at the campfire (Ch.12).

## Rites/customs referenced without extensive elaboration

- A blind/traditional "پیشگویی" (fortune-telling) stall is present at the Kolfarah gathering, run by an old man with a sheepskin. Evidence — Chapter 1.
- A rite involving a small round stone gripped and murmured over ("وِردی خواند") by the oldest man among the cave-guards, before the rescue. Evidence — Chapter 2.
- A "رسمِ پدرش" (father's custom) is referenced when Selo holds bread to the fire's flame before eating, at the Eshkaft-e-Salman campfire — no further explanation given. Evidence — Chapter 12.
- An old woman leaves bread by a spring "برای نارسینا" each spring when rain comes; stated as her personal practice, not universalized. Evidence — Chapter 6.
