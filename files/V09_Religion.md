## FACT: Deities as invoked in narrative dialogue/action

- **تیروتیر** — invoked in the Kolfarah inscription: "زیرا (خدای) تیروتیر مرا شایسته دانست." Evidence — Chapter 1. Also named on a banner/pennant carried by Hani's forces ("پرچمِ تیروتیر"), lowered at Hani's gesture during the ceasefire (Ch.8) and later reported lowered voluntarily by guards at Boluta'k during the "silence of the order" period (Ch.9). Club-men who attack the Khong-e-Azhdar assembly wear "گردنبندهایِ استخوانی با نشان تیروتیر و ناهونته." Evidence — Chapter 10.
- **هومبان، تیروتیر، ناپیر، شیموت** — invoked together in Shoterouro's formal reading of Hani's victory tablet in the temple: "خدای بزرگ هومبان، تیروتیر مسلح، ناپیر درخشنده، شیموت پیامآور خدایان، مرا یاری کردند تا ۲۰ تن از سرکردگان شورشیان را به اسارت درآورم و خونشان را به کفاره ریختم." Evidence — Chapter 1.
- **نارسینا** — the temple at Kolfarah where Nika plays and is arrested is dedicated to her. Evidence — Chapter 1; glossary. Women in the village ask Nika to "tell Narsina" to send more rain and lower the levy, half-joking: "ـ به نارسینا بگو بارون بیشتر بزنه. ... ـ و بگو خراج را هم کمتر کنه." Evidence — Chapter 1. An old woman leaves bread by a spring for Narsina every spring after rain, as her own stated personal practice. Evidence — Chapter 6.
- **تاریشا** — the shrine at Eshkaft-e-Salman is dedicated to her. Evidence — Chapter 12; glossary; Appendix. Hohin's meetings with people at Golshor are compared by her to sermons of "معبدهای نارسینا و تاریشا." Evidence — Chapter 3. An old woman from Pioun lights a small lamp at the "درگاهِ معبدِ تاریشا" before the Eshkaft-e-Salman assembly. Evidence — Chapter 12.
- **ناپیر** — invoked as a saying attributed to Manzat's mother: "به قول مادرم ناپیر: «روباه رو تو صحرا ول کن، راه خونه رو پیدا میکنه»." Evidence — Chapter 2. (Whether "ناپیر" here names the mother, or is being invoked as a proverb-source distinct from the mother's own name, is ambiguous in the text; recorded as written. Mother's given name, if different from "ناپیر": UNKNOWN.)
- **کیتِن** — footnoted as a distinct religious/political concept, not a deity: Evidence — Chapter 3 — Quote: "کیتِن: نیروی مقدس و فرّهی خدایان در باور ایالمیان؛ سرچشمهی مشروعیت و حمایت فرمانروایان." Invoked by an old traveler blaming a broken cart-axle on a "نفرینشده" pass because "کیتِن با ما نیست." Evidence — Chapter 3.
- **ناپهون (character)** — footnote glosses his personal name (not a deity name per se, but built from divine-root words): "به ایالمی: خدا: nap یا شکل بزرگتر آن napir + نور/روشنایی: hun." Evidence — Chapter 12. (See V02 Characters.)

## FACT: Appendix — additional historical/epigraphic notes touching religion

- The Kolfarah relief inscription explicitly credits Tirutir. Evidence — Appendix (reproducing Chapter 1's inscription).
- The Eshkaft-e-Salman relief inscription: "من، هانی، پیکر خود و هوهین، زن و خواهر محبوبم، و فرزندم را برای مادر تاریشا نصب کردم." Evidence — Appendix.
- Khong-e-Azhdar's worn Elamite-period relief is described (by the appendix) as likely depicting "بارعام یا گردهماییِ مردم در برابر شاه" (a public audience or gathering of people before the king) — stated as one of the oldest known depictions of "جمع شدنِ مردم" for political/ritual purposes; a separate, later Parthian-period relief on the same rock shows king Mehrdad II receiving legitimacy via two doves bearing a ring of power. Evidence — Appendix.
